[section]Scalation and shape[/section]

Four upper labials, with the second and third touching the eye; the fourth is the longest, the second is usually more than twice as wide as the third, the first is intermediate in length between the second and third. Five lower labials, the first three touching the anterior chin shields.

The rostral is usually higher than it is wide. The mental touches the anterior chin shields. There are three gulars in the midline between the posterior chin shields and the first ventral scale.

Preocular and postocular present; the preocular is lower than the eye diameter, the postocular is slightly lower than the eye diameter. Eye diameter equal to or smaller than the eye–to–mouth distance.

Prefrontal shorter than the frontal; length of frontal ca four-fifths of the parietal’s length; parietal ca 1.5 times as long as the prefrontal. Six scales surround the paraparietal. The prefrontal touches the first two upper labials.

Tail tapers in its posterior half; tail tip is not blunt. Length ratio [total length / tail length] in males 0.116 – 0.130 (n = 9), in females 0.074 – 0.087 (n = 9).

Ventrals in males: 121 – 142 (n = 9).
Subcaudals in males: 23 – 26 (n = 9).

Ventrals in females: 131 – 154 (n = 9).
Subcaudals in females: 16 – 20 (n = 9).

[section]Color and pattern[/section]

Dark brown or dark grey, with the posterior part of dorsal scales low on the sides – especially at the front of the body – yellow or pale, causing a “dotted” pattern. Higher up on the dorsum these light dots become smaller or may be lacking. Each ventral scale is dark-pigmented anteriorly and light posteriorly, causing a fine-banded belly pattern.

[section]Similar species[/section]

Scalation-wise, similar to C. battersbyi and C. borneensis. In C. borneensis, only the first two (not three) lower labials touch the anterior chin shield. Calamaria battersbyi is patterned differently, has a blunter tail tip, and – if one takes sex into consideration – has fewer subcaudals.

[section]Distribution[/section]

Known from the Indonesian part of Borneo, from West Kalimantan province to South Kalimantan and southern East Kalimantan provinces. The IUCN Red List assessment doubted its occurrence in West Kalimantan province, and mentioned (without any reference) a record from northern Sarawak. The photo below however seems to confirm its presence in the western part of West Kalimantan. All records are from the lowlands. It was until recently thought to be endemic to Borneo, but Sidik et al. (2018) recorded it from Karimunjawa Island, ca 70 km north of Java.
