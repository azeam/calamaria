Not all the terms listed in the glossary below are used in this app, but they may be used in other literature on Calamaria.

  • Adult Sexually mature
  • Allopatric Populations of two taxa living in non-overlapping areas are allopatric.
  • Anal The orifice at the base of the tail, although sometimes used to refer to the anal scale.
  • Anal scale The broad scale on the belly just before the anal opening (i.e., in front of the vent). In all Calamaria spp. it is “entire” (undivided; not divided in left and right halves).
  • Anterior Towards the front of the body.
  • ASL Above Sea Level (e.g., “up to 1500 m ASL”)
  • Caudal Referring to the tail.
  • cf. An abbreviation for “confer” (=compare with); used to indicate that the focal individual is similar to, but not necessarily conspecific with, the named taxon. Example of usage: Calamaria cf. hilleniusi.
  • Chin shields Two pairs of large scales under the chin (anterior vs. posterior chin shields).
  • Cloaca The cavity inside the anus.
  • Conspecific Belonging to the same species.
  • Congeneric Belonging to the same genus.
  • Crepuscular Active at dawn and dusk.
  • Cryptic species Two or more species that are difficult or impossible to distinguish between based on their morphology (including color and pattern), but different enough genetically to be considered separate species.
  • Dimorphism Occurring in two forms (two “morphs”).
  • Diurnal Active during the day.
  • Dorsal Referring to something on the back (dorsum).
  • Endemic Restricted to a certain area (e.g., “endemic to Borneo”).
  • Fossorial Burrowing; living underground.
  • Frontal A large scale (plate, scute) on the top of the head, between the eyes, that may be hexagonal or somewhat triangular in shape.
  • Gular Referring to the throat. Gular scales are those behind the posterior chin shields, in front of the first “proper” ventral.
  • Hemipenes The two halves of the bifurcated (=divided into two) penis of squamate reptiles. Only one hemipene is used when mating. When not in use, they are stored inverted in the base of the tail.
  • Hybridization Interbreeding, crossbreeding.
  • Internasals Two scales sitting on top of the snout, between the nostrils/nasals, in front of the prefrontals. These scales are missing in Calamaria spp.
  • Infralabials Lower labial (lip) scales.
  • Juvenile Not yet sexually mature.
  • Labial Lip scale.
  • Lateral Referring to something on the side.
  • Loreal A scale that sits above the upper labials, between the nostril and the eye or the preocular. Missing in Calamaria spp.
  • Mental The scale at the very front of the lower jaw, below where the tongue comes out.
  • Nasal The scale surrounding the nostril. In some snakes (such as Pseudorabdion spp.) it can be poorly defined and almost merged with the first upper labial. It may or may not be partially divided by a suture that reaches the nostril.
  • Nocturnal Active at night.
  • Nostril The opening through which the snake breathes.
  • Ocular Referring to the eye, possibly the transparent scale (“brille”) that covers the snake’s eye.
  • Ontogenetic Referring to some transition through the animal’s life (such as a gradual color change).
  • Oviparous Egg-laying.
  • Paraparietal A scale sitting behind/below the parietal, and behind /above the last upper labial.
  • Parapatric Populations that differ genetically and live in adjacent areas, with limited gene exchange in a narrow contact zone, are parapatric.
  • Parietals Two scales sitting behind the frontal, on the top/rear of the head.
  • Polymorphism Occurring in several forms.
  • Postocular Small scale sitting just behind the eye. In Calamaria spp. there is just one such scale or, rarely, none at all.
  • Prefrontal A pair of large scales sitting in front of the frontal. In Calamaria spp. these scales touch the rostral.
  • Preocular Small scale sitting just in front of the eye. In Calamaria spp. there is just one such scale or, rarely, none at all.
  • Rostral The scale at the very front of the upper jaw, above where the tongue comes out.
  • Scute Another word for scale; mostly used for larger scales (not dorsal scales).
  • sp. “species”, singular form; used when it is not clear which species (of a specified genus) it is.
  • spp. plural form of “sp.”
  • ssp. “subspecies”, singular form; used when it is not clear which subspecies it is.
  • sspp. plural form of “ssp.”
  • Subcaudal Literally, “under the tail”; the scales on the underside of the tail.
  • Supralabial Upper labial (lip) scale.
  • Supraocular Small scale above the eye. Present in all Calamaria spp., but may in some be restricted to the upper rear corner of the eye.
  • Suture Seam / border between two scales / scutes.
  • Sympatric Populations of taxa occupying the same geographic area are sympatric / live in sympatry.
  • Syntopic Like sympatric, but with co-occurrence at the exact same site (e.g., “C. schmidti and C. griswoldi are syntopic at the Kinabalu Park headquarters”).
  • Temporal Scales located in the temporal region, to the rear of the eye. Not often used when characterizing Calamaria spp., but the paraparietal and the smaller scales adjacent to it may be considered temporals.
  • Vent Often used casually, referring to something related to the cloaca; e.g. “snout-vent length” (distance of head+body, excluding the tail). Can also refer to the anal scale.
  • Venter Underside, belly.
  • Ventral Referring to the ventral scales, or to something on the belly (e.g., “ventral pattern”).
  • Ventrolateral Referring to the border between belly and side of the body.
  • Vertebral Referring to the center of the dorsum (back), usually the central dorsal scale row.
  • Viviparous Live-bearing.
