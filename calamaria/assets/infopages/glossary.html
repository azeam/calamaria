Not all the terms listed in the glossary below are used in this app, but they may be used in other literature on Calamaria.

Adult Sexually mature
Allopatric Populations of two taxa living in non-overlapping areas are allopatric.
Anal The orifice at the base of the tail, although sometimes used to refer to the anal scale.
Anal scaleThe broad scale on the belly just before the anal opening (i.e., in front of the vent). In all Calamaria spp. it is “entire” (undivided; not divided in left and right halves).
AnteriorTowards the front of the body.
ASLAbove Sea Level (e.g., “up to 1500 m ASL”)
CaudalReferring to the tail.
cf.An abbreviation for “confer” (=compare with); used to indicate that the focal individual is similar to, but not necessarily conspecific with, the named taxon. Example of usage: Calamaria cf. hilleniusi.
Chin shieldsTwo pairs of large scales under the chin (anterior vs. posterior chin shields).
CloacaThe cavity inside the anus.
ConspecificBelonging to the same species.
CongenericBelonging to the same genus.
CrepuscularActive at dawn and dusk.
Cryptic speciesTwo or more species that are difficult or impossible to distinguish between based on their morphology (including color and pattern), but different enough genetically to be considered separate species.
DimorphismOccurring in two forms (two “morphs”).
DiurnalActive during the day.
DorsalReferring to something on the back (dorsum).
EndemicRestricted to a certain area (e.g., “endemic to Borneo”).
FossorialBurrowing; living underground.
FrontalA large scale (plate, scute) on the top of the head, between the eyes, that may be hexagonal or somewhat triangular in shape.
GularReferring to the throat. Gular scales are those behind the posterior chin shields, in front of the first “proper” ventral.
HemipenesThe two halves of the bifurcated (=divided into two) penis of squamate reptiles. Only one hemipene is used when mating. When not in use, they are stored inverted in the base of the tail.
HybridizationInterbreeding, crossbreeding.
InternasalsTwo scales sitting on top of the snout, between the nostrils/nasals, in front of the prefrontals. These scales are missing in Calamaria spp.
InfralabialsLower labial (lip) scales.
JuvenileNot yet sexually mature.
LabialLip scale.
LateralReferring to something on the side.
LorealA scale that sits above the upper labials, between the nostril and the eye or the preocular. Missing in Calamaria spp.
MentalThe scale at the very front of the lower jaw, below where the tongue comes out.
NasalThe scale surrounding the nostril. In some snakes (such as Pseudorabdion spp.) it can be poorly defined and almost merged with the first upper labial. It may or may not be partially divided by a suture that reaches the nostril.
NocturnalActive at night.
NostrilThe opening through which the snake breathes.
OcularReferring to the eye, possibly the transparent scale (“brille”) that covers the snake’s eye.
OntogeneticReferring to some transition through the animal’s life (such as a gradual color change).
OviparousEgg-laying.
ParaparietalA scale sitting behind/below the parietal, and behind /above the last upper labial.
ParapatricPopulations that differ genetically and live in adjacent areas, with limited gene exchange in a narrow contact zone, are parapatric.
ParietalsTwo scales sitting behind the frontal, on the top/rear of the head.
PolymorphismOccurring in several forms.
PostocularSmall scale sitting just behind the eye. In Calamaria spp. there is just one such scale or, rarely, none at all.
PrefrontalA pair of large scales sitting in front of the frontal. In Calamaria spp. these scales touch the rostral.
PreocularSmall scale sitting just in front of the eye. In Calamaria spp. there is just one such scale or, rarely, none at all.
RostralThe scale at the very front of the upper jaw, above where the tongue comes out.
ScuteAnother word for scale; mostly used for larger scales (not dorsal scales).
sp.“species”, singular form; used when it is not clear which species (of a specified genus) it is.
spp.plural form of “sp.”
ssp.“subspecies”, singular form; used when it is not clear which subspecies it is.
sspp.plural form of “ssp.”
SubcaudalLiterally, “under the tail”; the scales on the underside of the tail.
SupralabialUpper labial (lip) scale.
SupraocularSmall scale above the eye. Present in all Calamaria spp., but may in some be restricted to the upper rear corner of the eye.
SutureSeam / border between two scales / scutes.
SympatricPopulations of taxa occupying the same geographic area are sympatric / live in sympatry.
SyntopicLike sympatric, but with co-occurrence at the exact same site (e.g., “C. schmidti and C. griswoldi are syntopic at the Kinabalu Park headquarters”).
TemporalScales located in the temporal region, to the rear of the eye. Not often used when characterizing Calamaria spp., but the paraparietal and the smaller scales adjacent to it may be considered temporals.
VentOften used casually, referring to something related to the cloaca; e.g. “snout-vent length” (distance of head+body, excluding the tail). Can also refer to the anal scale.
VenterUnderside, belly.
VentralReferring to the ventral scales, or to something on the belly (e.g., “ventral pattern”).
VentrolateralReferring to the border between belly and side of the body.
VertebralReferring to the center of the dorsum (back), usually the central dorsal scale row.
ViviparousLive-bearing.
